DistributionGroup

Article contributed by GilbertHerschberger (23 June 1999).

Introduction

Think "big". Start "small". Why does our ambition drive us to build the biggest, fastest, most complex software as release one? What is wrong with starting small?

The ideas behind JOS are big, really big. We are anticipating the development of a brand new kind of operating system. We're not just building an operating system in the tradition of UNIX(tm), Linux, or Microsoft Windows. There is something about JOS that is fundamentally different.

I am convinced of JOS. This is why I joined the JOS project. JOS, in theory, is technically superior to everything else. In the article IWantANewOS, Jonathan Knudsen agrees. He says that JOS has the potential to outperform even Linux as head-to-head competition to Microsoft Windows NT 5.0/2000.

Start small

I have immediate needs for all Java operating system. My ultra-conservative corporate customers are beginning to doubt that JOS is possible. My enthusiasm alone isn't going to keep them from soon spending millions on Microsoft. It's coming down to the wire. They must choose.

When they go ahead with their launch into Microsoft Windows 2000 (and Microsoft Office 2000), they are not going to look at another operating system again for five to seven years.

A small release of JOS would be better than no release at all. They want something tangible. They want to hold the JOS distribution CD-ROM in their hands. They doesn't want a finished product; they want hope.

Start big?

There are many reasons why a radically new approach to an operating system cannot start out big.

  1. There are too many new possibilities to consider. When we can do things in JOS that have never been done before, it takes time to figure out how to embrace this new capacity. We can't throw something together and limp along on bad analysis.

  2. No experience required. You can't find books on JOS. You can't find mentors for JOS. You can't hire a few JOS programmers to come in and help you out. Since JOS doesn't exist at this time, we are not yet experienced enough to know its strengths and weaknesses.

  3. Unlearn what we've been taught. When we don't have to do things the hard way, it takes time to get used to it. There are many techniques we must unlearn. Many tried and true approaches we may have used on other operating systems just won't work on JOS. It's a whole new game.

Hesitation

"He who hesitates is lost." When I look at the GNU Classpath website, I see a well organized open source project that people are enthusiastically contributing to. There was no hesitation on the part of the Classpath project about which license they were going to use. They use the LGPL for the Java API. They use the GPL for their corresponding test and example code.

Put compatibility first

Specifically, JOS must be backward compatible first in order to build a foundation to go forward. Step 1 is to build a release of JOS that we would otherwise consider to be child's play. Throw something together. It doesn't have to be pretty. It doesn't have to be perfect. It just has to exist.

For example, we must be able to at least read .conf and .ini files. There are a lot of existing .conf and .ini files out there from other operating systems. If we are able to read them easily... 

String url = "x-registry:file:X1186Config";
Registry r = RegistryFactory.getRegistry( url );
...we import data from .conf and .ini files into our own JOS registry. We can help programmers everywhere port versions of their device driver to run on JOS.

Help wanted

We must get help. We cannot write all of the device drivers by ourselves. We must not think of solving the device driver problem by spending our own time writing something we're not qualified to write. We must provide a JOS Driver Development Kit (JOS-DDK) for other programmers to write device drivers.

We need their help to refine the JOS driver API and the JOS-DDK. When we think big, it means that our product will outperform even Linux and Microsoft Windows 2000. When we start small, we can get great feedback from the entire Internet community to refine and mature our JOS API.
