Introduction

Dispatcher is a buzzword. Like a dispatcher in the real world, a dispatcher in a program is often very busy. A dispatcher is ofter where all of the support calls are coming in. An emergency 911 dispatcher, for example, is far too busy recieving calls to actually rescue people personally.

On a busy day down at our local 911 center, there are half a dozen people. They are waiting by the phone, ready to answer it if it rings. Collectively, they are responsible for over one hundred emergency vehicles and up to three hundred emergency personnel. When you dial 911, they assess the emergency, prioritize and classify it, and send out just the right people and equipment. Soon after the call comes in, the dispatcher is out of the picture. The emergency equipment rolls. Each emergency is turned over to people in the field.

A dispatcher in software has a similar role. A dispatcher is waiting for some event. It assesses the event, prioritizes and classifies it, and sends it on to some other method.

Why do we need dispatchers?

There are many practical reasons.

  • Services are subject to change. Regular people, like myself, can't keep up with how local emergency service is organized. There are dozens of telephone numbers to remember.
  • Dispatchers are given a well-known name. No matter what kind of emergency it is, people can dial one telephone number. Dispatchers are trained to send each kind of emergency to each kind of emergency service.
  • Dispatchers control limited resources. It just isn't feasible for each person to hunt down the closest available fire truck for themselves. Our local center only has a few fire trucks. A dispatcher assignes trucks to fires. If there is a fire, the dispatcher finds the closest one. In a crisis, the dispatcher finds a truck from surrounding communities.

We need dispatchers in software for similar reasons. A dispatcher controls limited resources, like a keyboard and mouse. There is only one real keyboard and mouse connected to my computer. When there are so many programs running at the same time, it just isn't feasible for each program to use the real keyboard and mouse directly. The keyboard must be virtualized by a multiple process operating system.

The operating system is responsible for the virtualization of all limited resources. The dispatcher is a great way to make this happen.
