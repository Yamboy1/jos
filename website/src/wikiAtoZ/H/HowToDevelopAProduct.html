Prerequisites

  • Choose your license: public domain, LGPL or open source.
  • Choose your product.
  • Decide if your going to let others look at the source code.

Procedure

This is good advice any time you're building a product, especially an open source product.

  1. Build a simple product based on your idea. Build only a small part. Build only as much as you can. Test it extensively.
  2. Get your product and ideas into circulation. "Release early. Release often" -- from Cathedral and Bazaar. Package it in a .zip or .jar file. Upload it as soon as possible to your own web site.
  3. Make a download page about in Wiki. Think about adding a group page, a directory page, or a discussion page to Wiki as interest in your product (hopefully) grows. Put your ideas into an HTML document and upload it to your own website. When posting to reference documents to Wiki, you can include an HTML link to your document. Go back to step 1.
  4. E-mail people one-on-one for their feedback. Look on the resource pages for people who might be interested in your product. When you get feedback you can use, go back to step 1.
  5. Announce it on an appropriate mailing list. When you participate in discussions on a mailing list, look for ways to improve your product and remain true to its original scope. When a new product has been identified, start a separate project.
  6. Discuss with other members of the JOS project how your product might fit into a standard JOS distribution. When the discussion has reached a concensus, you can port your product to the jos or org.jos namespace. Members of the JOS project must vote for packages to include in the standard distributions.
  7. Ask for someone to mirror your .zip files to the jos.org server.

About releases

Choose a good name for your distribution files. Provide a unique name for each .zip or .jar you release. Don't use a name like product.zip. Make a file name that lasts, like jos-product-1.0.3.zip.

In an internet project, you should expect to keep a release on your website for at least one year. Plan on it. There are good reasons for other programmers to look back at old releases to understand where your product is coming from.

When editing your Wiki page, you should make HTML links to a .zip or .jar on your own website.
